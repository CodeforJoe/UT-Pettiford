Conclusion

 

            Oscar Pettiford clearly stood out among his peers in the mid-1940�s and 1950�s as one of the first bassists to adapt the bebop vocabulary to the double bass. In doing this, Pettiford earned the admiration of numerous bassists including Ray Brown, Charles Mingus and Paul Chambers. Pettiford, after Jimmy Blanton, was one of the most important figures in establishing the bass as a capable and respected solo instrument in jazz.

            Pettiford�s work as a bassist, alone, would probably have been sufficient to distinguish him from others during the bebop era, but his work as a composer and bandleader was also remarkable. The bassist-bandleader was and still is a relatively unusual figure in jazz. In these capacities, Pettiford set musical precedents for such groups as those of Charles Mingus, Paul Chambers, and, more recently, John Patitucci, Dave Holland, and Avishai Cohen. As the leader of his own groups, Pettiford gave himself a platform from which to highlight and develop his talent on an instrument known primarily it's supporting role. These settings also allowed him to perform on the cello, which he used as a higher pitched extension of his bass.

            The fact that the American Federation of Musicians� recording ban prohibited any recorded documentation of Pettiford�s participation in the earliest bebop ensembles has caused his contributions to early bebop ensembles be unnoticed by many. His expatriation to Europe, and premature death in 1960, were factors that further vitiated his reputation among many musicians. 

It is quite possible that Pettiford�s greatest influence on the jazz world today has been indirect. Among many others influenced by Pettiford, some discussed in more detail above, is Ray Brown, who achieved prominence with the Oscar Peterson trio in the 1950�s and 1960�s, Paul Chambers and Ron Carter, who gained exposure with Miles Davis� groups. Scores of influential and accomplished bassists, including Sam Jones, Buster Williams, George Mraz, Ray Brown, Chuck Israels and Larry Grenadier have given Pettiford credit for influencing their sound and style in some way. It is significant, further, that Pettiford�s music is now a required part of the repertoire for many aspiring jazz bass students.

Pettiford�s extensive discography as a sideman and a leader has been reissued and is now widely available in various formats. This has allowed more recent generations of bassists to be informed by his playing. A clear and accurate discography of his recorded output, however, does not exist; and no major written work has exclusively devoted to his historical contributions to jazz.

            This study of Oscar Pettiford�s solo style and its relation to that of other bassists of the bebop era show that for his time, Pettiford had few peers.
