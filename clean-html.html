Foreword

Oscar Pettiford was one of the most important and innovative jazz bass soloists in the 1940's and 1950's. Pettiford further developed the "horn-like" approach of bass soloing first pioneered by Jimmy Blanton, by transferring the language and phrasing of bebop onto the double bass. In doing this, Pettiford was an early prototype of the "modern" jazz bassist. In his hands, the double bass became an instrument capable of a high level of artistic expression, utilizing many of the same melodic devices as the best jazz wind players of his day. Pettiford's influence has been profound, although often indirect, and traces of his style can be found in the styles of many modern jazz bassists.

This paper draws on the analysis of transcribed bass solos, as well as historical accounts, to establish Pettiford's accomplishments as a soloist, examine his style in relation to his peers, and discuss his influence and relevance to the styles of three modern bass soloists.

Chapter 1: Background

Early Life (1922-1943)

Oscar Pettiford (1922-1960) was born on an Indian reservation in Okmulgee, Oklahoma, into a large musical family. [1] Pettiford received his early musical training and musical experience in the family band, Doc Pettiford and His Family Orchestra. The band featured his father, "Doc" Pettiford, and included his mother and his ten siblings. [2] Pettiford began dancing with the band at age six, later playing the piano, trombone and trumpet before taking up the bass. [3] Pettiford was educated in the basics of music theory by his older sister, Leontine, who played piano and reeds and who arranged most of the band's music. [4] The family band toured the southern and midwestern United States before eventually basing itself in Minneapolis, Minnesota, where Pettiford was raised.

Pettiford encountered many influential musicians at after-hours jam sessions in Minneapolis. It was in one of these sessions where he met Milt Hinton, bassist with Cab Calloway, [5] jammed with the electric guitar pioneer Charlie Christian, and had a "cutting contest" [Jb᠊뿿윀1] with Jimmy Blanton, bassist with the Duke Ellington Orchestra and the progenitor of the modern jazz bass solo. [6]

In the early 1940's, musical work was scarce in Minneapolis. Pettiford briefly gave up professional music and went to work in a war plant while studying tailoring. He returned to professional music at the urging of bassist Milt Hinton. [7] Pettiford's first major opportunity in jazz came in 1943 when saxophonist and bandleader Charlie Barnet hired him to be part of a two-bass team [Jb᠊뿿윀2] with Chubby Jackson. Pettiford only stayed with Barnet for five months, however, and this role was undocumented because of an American Federation of Musicians' recording ban in effect at the time. [8] He was dismissed when his competitive nature and abrasive personality combined to cause friction between himself and bassist Chubby Jackson. [9] After his departure from Barnet's band, Pettiford relocated and settled in New York City. [10] It was here that he immediately became part of the nascent bebop scene, which first developed in Harlem and then later took hold at several nightclubs located on 52nd [Jb᠊뿿윀3] Street, between 5th and 6th avenue.

Early Career in New York City (1943-1945)

In New York, Pettiford's first gig was with pianist and bebop pioneer Thelonious Monk at Minton's Playhouse in Harlem. Minton's Playhouse was the setting for some of the earliest bebop jam sessions attended by musicians such as Dizzy Gillespie, Charlie Parker, Jimmy Blanton and Charlie Christian. In June of 1943, Pettiford worked for a short time at the Onyx Club on 52nd Street with famed trumpeter Roy Eldridge, who ultimately fired Pettiford because of [11] his [Jb᠊뿿윀4] constant tardiness and his drinking. [12]

Notwithstanding these setbacks, Pettiford's reputation as a bassist grew quickly, and by the end of 1943 he had won first place in the bass category of Esquire magazine's All-American Jazz Band Poll. [13] The winners of this poll, which included Louis Armstrong, Jack Teagarden, Sid Catlett, and Coleman Hawkins, performed a concert that was recorded and released as The First Esquire Concert (Laser Light 15 723).

Due largely to the success of the Esquire award, Pettiford was able to book a gig on 52nd Street at the Onyx Club as co-leader with trumpeter Dizzy Gillespie, pianist George Wallington, tenor saxophonist Don Byas and drummer Max Roach. [14] This group, which was also unrecorded, is now recognized as the first bebop ensemble on 52nd Street, a strip of nightclubs that would become, for a short time, a bastion for bebop musicians. Pettiford took a direct role in the musical direction of this band, and according to Budd Johnson, a saxophonist who performed with the group, half of this group's repertoire was written by Pettiford. "One Bass Hit," which was then known as "For Bass Faces Only," was included among these works. [15]

In 1944, after just a few months of work in New York City, Pettiford's heavy drinking and confrontational personality caused a falling-out between him and Gillespie, which, in turn, caused the breakup of the ensemble. Following that band's dissolution, Pettiford led various bands on 52nd Street, eventually playing and recording withThe Boyd Raeburn Band (1944-45)[16] [17] [Jb᠊뿿윀5] before traveling to California to perform with legendary saxophonist Coleman Hawkins. Pettiford appeared with Hawkins in the 1945 film, The Crimson Canary, although another bassist overdubbed the bass part heard in the movie. [18] Pettiford also recorded as a leader of an eighteen-piece group in 1945 for the Manor record label under the name Oscar Pettiford and His 18 All-Stars. [19]

Time with Duke Ellington (1945-1948)

In November of 1945, Pettiford was hired to fill the bass chair in the Duke Ellington Orchestra, succeeding bassist Jimmy Blanton, who died in 1942.[20] Pettiford performed and recorded with Ellington from 1945 through March of 1948, returning to the ensemble after that for occasional performances. [21] Near the end of his time with the Ellington band, however, Pettiford grew restless with them, only enjoying the concerts "because then we'd get to play some new things. At dances and theaters we'd play the same things over and over again. There was nothing to inspire you." [22]

1948-1950

After leaving Ellington, Pettiford worked at The Three Deuces on 52nd Street first with pianist Erroll Garner and later with pianist George Shearing, trombonist Kai Winding, and trumpeter Miles Davis (1948). [23] Pettiford also led a short-lived band at The Clique nightclub that included Miles Davis, Fats Navarro, Lucky Thompson, Dexter Gordon, Milt Jackson, Bud Powell and Kenny Clarke.[24] This ensemble also disbanded as a result of conflict between Pettiford and the other musicians. [25]

In 1949, Pettiford joined Woody Herman's "Second Herd" band. During his time with the Herman band, Pettiford broke his arm playing baseball. Before he broke his arm but during his time with Herman, Pettiford experimented with the cello as part of a prank on the bandleader. After his arm was broken, he was unable to play bass due to the cast. During this time, Pettiford instead devoted his energy to practicing the cello, which he tuned in fourths and played pizzicato, essentially as a smaller bass sounding an octave higher than the conventional jazz bass. [Jb᠊뿿윀6]

Bandleader and Sideman (1950-1958)

During the early 1950's Pettiford continued working as a bandleader, engaging in an ill-fated State Department tour and making recordings on both bass and cello for Charles Mingus's Debut record label, the Roost record label, and others. [26] During this period, Pettiford also worked with Charlie Shavers and Louis Bellson (1953) and in Bud Powell's trio (1953). [27]

In 1955, Pettiford was hired to lead a band at the Café Bohemia. Pettiford's group was a last minute replacement for one led by Charlie Parker, who died before his scheduled opening. [28] Pettiford led a band that included trombonist Jimmy Cleveland, saxophonist Jerome Richardson, pianist Horace Silver, drummer Kenny Clarke and alto saxophonist Julian "Cannonball" Adderley, who was discovered after Pettiford was tricked into letting him onto the bandstand to sit in. [29]

Pettiford recorded with Miles Davis on his 1955 album, The Musings of Miles (Original Jazz Classics OJC-004). The band featured on this recording, minus Pettiford, would eventually form the nucleus of Davis' classic mid-fifties quintet. According to Red Garland, Davis originally sought to have Garland, Oscar Pettiford, Sonny Rollins and Max Roach make up his working quintet. [30] When Davis was ready to put his own band together, however, Pettiford, Rollins and Roach were already working with their own groups and were, therefore, unavailable.[31] Paul Chambers, a gifted young bassist inspired by both Blanton and Pettiford was instead hired. Chambers would remain with Davis's groups for eight years and in this position influence countless other bassists.

From the mid 1950's until leaving for Europe in 1958, Pettiford recorded prolifically both as a leader and as a sideman with both large ensembles and numerous small combos. In 1956, Pettiford assembled a thirteen-piece big band, which debuted at New York's Town Hall. This band, the Oscar Pettiford Orchestra, played together for eighteen months and featured a group of seasoned musicians that included Ernie Royal, Art Farmer, Jimmy Cleveland, Gig Gryce, Lucky Thompson, Benny Golson, Art Farmer, Tommy Flanagan, David Amram, and Osie Johnson, among others.[32] This group featured Pettiford on both the cello (which in some instances was amplified with an added electronic tremolo effect) and bass, at times simultaneously via overdubbing. This group also expanded the traditional instrumentation of the jazz big band by employing two French horns and a harpist as regular members. [Jb᠊뿿윀7]

As a sideman, Pettiford contributed to several notable recordings during this time, including Thelonious Monk'sMonk Plays Ellington (1955), Brilliant Corners (1956 ) and Sonny Rollins' Freedom Suite, (1958) which featured Rollins, Pettiford and Max Roach as a piano-less trio.

Final Years in Europe (1958-1960)

Pettiford left New York City for Europe in 1958 as part of a Jazz from Carnegie Hall package tour. [33] He toured France and Germany and eventually settled in Copenhagen, Denmark. [34] In Copenhagen Pettiford worked with such other American expatriate jazz musicians as Stan Getz, Kenny Clarke, and Bud Powell as well as with various European musicians, including guitarist Attilla Zoller, trumpeter Alan Botschinky, pianist Jan Johansson, and drummer Hans Koller.[35] [36] According to bassist Dave Friesen, the Danish jazz bass virtuoso Niels-Henning Orsted Peterson, who lived in Copenhagen, mentioned studying with Pettiford.[37] It was in Copenhagen during 1959 and 1960 that Pettiford made some of his final recordings, issued as Montmartre Blues, using a band comprised of European musicians. Oscar Pettiford died on September 8, 1960, in Copenhagen, Denmark, from a "flu-like virus" following a concert at an art gallery. [38]


Chapter II: Analysis of Pettiford's Solos

Overview

In the history of the double bass as a solo instrument in jazz, Oscar Pettiford was a bridge linking the work of Jimmy Blanton and the generations of jazz bassists that followed him. Pettiford stood out from many of his contemporaries in the 1940's and 1950's, building on Blanton's accomplishments as a soloist and discarding the heavier root-oriented approach to soloing typical of many of his contemporaries. Pettiford was able to both translate and transfer much of the vocabulary and conventions of bebop including the use of chromaticism, "horn-like" phrasing, scales/arpeggios and altered pitches to the bass.

2.1: "Burt's Pad"

This solo on "Burt's Pad" is included on disc two of the collection Oscar Rides Again (Proluxe 5002). It was recorded on March 13, 1954. Present with Pettiford on this recording are Kai Winding, trombone; Al Cohn, tenor sax; Henri Renaud, piano; Tal Farlow, guitar; and Max Roach, drums.

Phrasing

One of the most remarkable aspects of Pettiford's solos is his phrasing. In this solo, as is characteristic of his style, Pettiford uses "horn-like" lines consisting primarily of eighth-notes, which he builds into lengthy multi-measure statements. In mm. 1-4, Pettiford plays two phrases, each two measures long. The third and concluding phrase, mm. 5-9, is five measures long and overlaps the beginning of the second "A" section.

Example 1: Phrasing in mm. 1-9 of "Burt's Pad."

Avoidance of Roots and Fifths as Prominent Pitches in Solos

By his avoidance of the heavy use of roots and fifths, Pettiford breaks with the common tendency of bassists to outline the same pitches required of walking bass lines. This, along with the inclusion of the upper extensions of the harmony, often within arpeggios, gives his solos harmonic depth, and a sense of motion often lacked by other solo bassists during his time. [Jb᠊뿿윀8]

In this solo, Pettiford avoids placing the root or fifth of the harmony on the downbeats of chord changes. In m. 1, he plays the major seventh two times, on the downbeat of beats one and three, while placing the root on the upbeat of one and the fifth on the upbeat of two. In m. 3, Pettiford plays the second (or ninth) on beat one, then outlines the C Major chord from the third to the ninth. The third phrase, although it begins in m. 4 with a root, anticipates the following measure's harmony by half of a beat causing the ninth to land on the downbeat of m. 5. [Jb᠊뿿윀9] Pettiford does however, use chord roots prominently in m. 4 along with a flatted ninth as a chromatic upper neighbor.

2.2: Analysis of "Perdido"

Overview

"Perdido," from Oscar Rides Again (Proluxe 5002), was recorded in New York City on June 12, 1956, by Pettiford's big band the Oscar Pettiford Orchestra. In this solo, as is characteristic of his general approach, Pettiford employs long eighth-note lines, arpeggios, chromaticism and motivic development.

Phrasing

In this solo, as in "Burt's Pad," Pettiford achieves a sense of fluidity by using long, flowing phrasing. In mm. 1-7 of this solo, Pettiford employs a three-phrase statement, clearly punctuating the end of each phrase with a dotted quarter note in mm. 3, 5, 7. The first two phrases of this statement occur in mm. 1-3 and mm. 3-5, and are each eight beats long, beginning on the "and" of two and ending on the downbeat of beat one. The third and concluding phrase, in mm. 5-7, while also beginning on the "and" of two, is two beats longer and ends on an upbeat. Pettiford also varies his phrase endings by changing the direction and interval in each case. The first phrase ends with a descending minor second interval, the second an ascending major second and the third with an ascending minor third.

Example 10: Phrase development in mm. 1-7 of "Perdido."

Recurring Motives

Pettiford also creates cohesion in this solo by using recurring motives. In mm. 1-2, 5-6, and 13 he plays an arpeggio figure over the C min7 that includes the third, fifth, seventh and ninth. In mm. 2 and 6, he appears to extend the Cmin7 harmony until beat three of the measure when he sounds the pitch A, the third of the F7 chord.

Example 11: Recurring motive over Cmin7 in mm. 1-2, 5-6 and 13 of "Perdido."

Pettiford uses a similar descending motive three times in this solo, over G7 in m. 8, F7 in m. 10, and Cmin7 in m. 29. Consistent with the phrase development just discussed, Pettiford augments the phrase length and changes the ending interval in each use. He also moves the motive closer to the beginning of the measure each time he uses it. In the first occurrence of this motive, mm. 8-9, the phrase is six beats long and ends with a descending minor second. The second, in mm. 10-11, is six and a half beats long and ends with an ascending major second. The third and concluding iteration of this motive, mm. 29-32, occurs near the end of the form. It is part of a four-measure phrase that ends with a descending minor second.

Example 12: First instance of descending motive in mm. 8-9 of "Perdido."

Example 13: Second instance of recurring descending motive in mm. 10-11 of "Perdido."

Example 14: Third and concluding instance of recurring descending motive in mm. 29-32 of "Perdido."

Arpeggios as a Recurring Motive

Pettiford plays a similar motive over both the Dmin7 and G7 chords in mm. 4, 12, and 32. In m. 4, he arpeggiates the lower three tones of the Dmin7 chord then plays the flatted ninth (as an upper chromatic neighbor), fifth, root and seventh of G7. In m. 12 Pettiford plays the complete descending Dmin7 arpeggio followed the same G7 pattern as m. 4. In m. 32, he appears to anticipate the G7 by two beats with third, fifth, seventh and flatted ninth used again as an upper chromatic neighbor tone.

Example 15: Recurring motive in mm. 4, 12, 32 in "Perdido."

Lyrical Idea Marking Form of Tune

Although Pettiford overlaps a phrase across the A and B sections of the tune, he marks the arrival of the bridge in m. 17 by playing a lyrical idea containing the root, third, and fifth of the D7 chord. He also does something similar to this in his solo on "Burt's Pad."

Example 16: Lyrical phrase outlining the bridge in mm. 14-17 of "Perdido."

Non-Harmonic Tones

As discussed previously in the analysis of "Burt's Pad," Pettiford's solos stand out because of his use of chromatic passing tones to create smooth, flowing solo lines. In this solo, Pettiford employs nine chromatic passing tones, each of which occurs only as part of descending lines. These passing tones occur in mm. 2, 5, 8, 9, 10, 11, 21, 29 and 30.

Example 17: Chromatic passing tones in mm. 29-30 of "Perdido."

Blues language

Pettiford implies the blues sound with a Db, the flatted third of the key, in m. 14. Although this pitch is also the raised fifth of the prevailing F7 harmony, Pettiford slides into it from below implying the Bb blues sound. He returns to the Bb blues sound again in m. 18, playing both the major and minor (flatted) third of Bb over the D7 harmony. In example 19, Pettiford plays an Eb7 arpeggio over the F7 harmony in m. 24. The resulting pitches, G, Bb, and Db, clearly introduce the blues language motive that Pettiford then develops through repetition in mm. 25-28. The use of blues language on non-blues tunes is a practice also associated with bassist Ray Brown, who listed Pettiford as one of the two greatest influences on his music, the other being Jimmy Blanton. [39]

Summary

Pettiford, as evidenced in these solos, possessed a highly developed solo style. In his solos he broke free from many of the melodic patterns associated with bass lines, incorporating many of the devices associated with the bebop horn players of his day: multi-measure eighth-note phrases, arpeggios, chromatic passing tones, chromatic upper and lower neighbor tones, and altered tones. The fact that he performed on other instruments before the bass may have been a factor leading to his adoption of the "horn-like" approach to soloing on the bass. Pettiford's ability to translate the vocabulary of bebop to the bass is even more remarkable considering his lack of formal training on the instrument. In Pettiford's time, relatively few jazz bassists had access to formal instruction; this lack of technical training undoubtedly hindered many from developing sophisticated solo styles. Furthermore, the instrument's setup prior to the development of electronic amplification and steel strings created enormous difficulties in projecting the sound of the bass through an ensemble. Much of bass technique at this time focused on producing adequate volume at the expense of other qualities.

Pettiford's ability to achieve a relatively high level of musical fluency on the instrument, despite his lack of training and the inherent physical difficulties presented by the bass, is a testament to his innate musical abilities. His solos, which are remarkable on their own merits, stand out even further when compared to the solos of many of his contemporaries in the bebop scene of the late 1940's and early 1950's.

Chapter III: Analysis of Pettiford's peers

Overview

The following solos were selected for analysis due to their inclusion on recordings made by alto saxophonist Charlie Parker, one of the founders of bebop. These recordings, made between 1947 and 1950, indicate that many bassists of the bebop era were not, at first, able to assimilate the genre's melodic and harmonic concepts into their solos.

3.1 Analysis of Tommy Potter's solo on "Bongo Bop"

Bassist Tommy Potter (1918-1988) was a member of Charlie Parker's band from 1947 to 1950; and he worked with many bebop musicians, including Fats Navarro, Wardell Gray, Stan Getz and Bud Powell. [40] Potter's solos do not reflect any attempt to translate the solo language of bebop into that of the bass, and are in stark contrast to Pettiford's more highly developed solo style. Of the twenty-six tracks Potter recorded with Charlie Parker, as collected on The Complete Savoy & Dial Master Takes, Potter "solos" on ten. Each one is essentially a walking bass line with light accompaniment from the rest of the rhythm section. This bass solo on "Bongo Bop" (SVY 17149) was recorded in 1947 with the Charlie Parker Quintet and is representative of Potter's usual approach. Featured on this recording is Miles Davis, trumpet; Charlie Parker, alto sax; Duke Jordan, piano; Max Roach, drums.

Example 20: Tommy Potter's solo on "Bongo Bop"

Instead of in any sense being solos themselves, Potter's solo breaks serve more as textural contrast between the previous solos and the recapitulation of the tune's melody. Indeed, his solos merit discussion only because they appear on recordings made by Charlie Parker and others pioneers of the bebop style.

3.2 Red Callendar's solo on "Cool Blues"

Overview

Red Callendar (1916-1992) was a Los Angeles bassist who recorded as a member of the Charlie Parker Quintet during Parker's time in Los Angeles, and he was among Charles Mingus' first bass teachers. Callendar solos with a heavier, more root-oriented approach that seems to relate more to the tuba, which he also played. Callendar recorded "Cool Blues" in 1947 with Charlie Parker's quintet in Hollywood, California, on The Complete Savoy & Dial Master (SVY 17149). Featured on this recording are Charlie Parker on alto sax, Erroll Garner on piano, and Harold "Doc" West on drums.

Roots and Fifths as Prominent Note Choices

Unlike Pettiford, Callendar favors the root and fifth of the harmony as prominent notes in this solo. He emphasizes these pitches at the beginning and end of phrases in mm. 1-6, 9-11, 11-12, 13-16, 17-20, 21-22.

Example 21: Root and fifth as prominent pitches in mm. 1-4 of "Cool Blues."

Example 22: Root and fifth as prominent pitches in mm. 5-6 of "Cool Blues."

Non-Harmonic Tones

Callendar's use of chromaticism in this solo is extremely limited. He uses only one chromatic passing tone, and one chromatic lower neighbor tone, which can be found in mm. 12 and 19 respectively.

Example 23: Chromatic passing tone and chromatic lower neighbor tone in mm. 12 and 19 of "Cool Blues."

Callendar uses wider intervals, including a ninth in m. 21, and he seems to imply a tri-tone substitution in m. 22 by using the flatted ninth and raised fifth of G7. Due to the relatively wide interval between these two notes, Callendar's use of these altered tones are not "horn-like" but are instead more idiomatic to the fingerboard layout of the double bass.

Example 24: Root, fifth, ninth and octave in a descending sequence in mm. 21-22 of "Cool Blues."

Callendar's limited use of chromaticism, combined with his preference for arpeggio patterns favoring roots and fifths, give this solo a heavy "chugging" feel that is more tuba-like and far less fluid and bebop-oriented than Pettiford's style.

3.2 Nelson Boyd's solo on "Half Nelson"

Bassist Nelson Boyd (1928-1985) worked with Dizzy Gillespie, Coleman Hawkins, Dexter Gordon, Thelonious Monk, Miles Davis, and Charlie Parker. [41] He takes a one-chorus solo on his namesake tune "Half Nelson," which was recorded on August 14, 1947, by the Miles Davis All Stars on The Complete Savoy & Dial Master Takes (SVY 17149). Present on this recording are Miles Davis, trumpet; Charlie parker, alto sax; John Lewis, piano; Max Roach, drums.

Boyd also uses a more rudimentary approach to bass soloing as compared to Pettiford, relying largely on roots and fifths as prominent pitches in this solo. Boyd uses the root or fifth as the first and last note of his phrases in mm. 3-8, 9-10, and mm. 11-14.

Example 25: Roots as prominent notes in mm. 3-6 of "Half Nelson."

Non-Harmonic Pitches

In this solo, Boyd uses only two chromatic devices common to bebop. In m. 1, he plays a chromatic lower neighbor to approach the third of C major. In measure 12, he plays a chromatic passing tone between the sixth and fifth of the D7 chord.

Example 26: Chromatic lower neighbor and chromatic passing tones in mm. 1 and 12 of "Half Nelson."

Phrasing and Motivic Development

In comparison to Pettiford, Boyd's rhythmic and motivic development is also less developed. Unlike Pettiford, who varied the length, placement, and intervallic direction of his lines, Boyd relies exclusively on short phrases that do not increase in length or cross the internal boundaries of the tune as established by the chord changes. Boyd also does not make use of any recurring melodic or rhythmic motives to create a sense of structural unity in the solo. He does, however use a sequence in the last two measures of the tune, mm. 15-16. It is a descending sequence, which can be played by moving the same fingering pattern and hand position down the neck in half steps. He ignores the AbMaj7 chord in measure 15, instead planing this pattern downward to create a descending upper and lower chromatic line.

Example 27: Sequence in mm. 15-16 of "Half Nelson."

3.3 Curley Russell's Solo on "Mohawk"

Curley Russell (1917-1986) was a contemporary of Oscar Pettiford who worked with many of the same musicians. Russell worked with groups led by Tadd Dameron, Bud Powell, Horace Silver, and Art Blakey, and he was a member of Dizzy Gillespie and Charlie Parker's 1945 quintet. [42] Although he was a proficient accompanist, his solos also fall short of the standard set by Pettiford. Dizzy Gillespie in To Be Or Not to Bop recalls: "…Curley got around, but his solos weren't in the vein of Jimmy Blanton, Oscar Pettiford, and Ray Brown." [43]

"Mohawk" was recorded in 1950 for the album Bird and Diz (Verve compact disc 314 521 436-2). Present on this recording are Dizzy Gillespie, trumpet; Charlie Parker, alto saxophone; Thelonious Monk, piano; Buddy Rich, drums. While Russell displays a more developed melodic and rhythmic sense than do the other bassists discussed so far, his articulation is heavy and his tone lacks the clarity of Pettiford's.

Russell's preference for using the root and the fifth as a starting or ending note adds to this heaviness. In mm.1-2, Russell begins and ends his first phrase with a root Bb, and ends the phrase with the same note, which is the fifth of the Eb7 chord. Russell's note choices in mm. 1-2, which do not clearly outline the seventh of or third of Eb7 in m. 2, suggest that he is blanketing the Bb major scale. He also ends phrases with the root in mm. 6, 7, and with a fifth in m. 12.

Example 28: Root and fifth as prominent notes in mm. 1-2 of "Mohawk."

Altered Pitches

Russell plays two altered pitches in this solo and in each instance, these tones are used either as part of arpeggios or as the final pitch in a phrase, rather than as part of any stepwise or scalar motion. In m. 6, he plays the raised fifth as part of a descending Eb7#5 arpeggio. In m. 10, Russell plays the flatted fifth over the F7 harmony.

Example 29: Altered pitches in mm. 6 and 10 of "Mohawk."

Non-Harmonic Pitches

The absence of chromatic passing tones, neighbor tones, or enclosures in this solo make it incongruous with the presence of bebop innovators Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie on the recording.

Summary

The analysis of the solo styles of these bassists helps to provide the historical context needed to properly evaluate Pettiford's accomplishments. These solos give evidence of the degree to which many of Pettiford's peers had not yet fully assimilated the language of bebop into their solo styles. These solos, excepting Potter's, do contain some of the elements associated with bebop, including eighth-note phrases, chromaticism, altered-pitches and syncopation. However, none of these bassists were able to fully integrate these elements into a solo style that could be considered "horn-like." These solos demonstrate that Pettiford, for his time, had developed a very advanced improvisational style on the bass.

Chapter IV: Analysis of Three Modern Bass Soloists

Overview

Oscar Pettiford's recordings and compositions have influenced many jazz bassists both during his lifetime and after his death in 1960. Since that time, jazz bassists have achieved much higher levels of technical proficiency on the instrument; most often, this has been the result of formal music study. The characteristics of the modern jazz sound include a bright and sustaining tone, the expanded use of the high register or "thumb position" for soloing, and the use of two-finger pizzicato to play fast "horn-like" solo improvisations, often with legato phrasing. In order to achieve this sound the majority of modern jazz bassists also make use of steel strings, reduced string height (in comparison to players of the 1950's), and since the 1970's, electronic amplification that allows them to project clearly in all registers of the instruments. It should be noted that Pettiford was experimenting with amplification and electronic effects as early as the 1950's. The three bassists selected for analysis are all virtuosos who represent three subsequent generations of jazz bassists who have sought to develop the bass as a melodic instrument.

Although it is impossible to determine the total extent of Pettiford's (or any other musician's) influence on these bassists, each have followed Pettiford's model and brought the bass to the forefront as a solo voice. In their efforts to create solos of the caliber of the greatest horn players in jazz, they have undoubtedly drawn not only on other bassists for inspiration, but directly from other instrumentalists as well.

4.1 Scott LaFaro

Scott LaFaro (1936-1961) is best known for his work as a member of the Bill Evans Trio. Like Pettiford, LaFaro is recognized as one of the most important figures in the development of double bass after Jimmy Blanton, Oscar Pettiford and Ray Brown. [44] In addition to being the first well-known "modern" bass soloist in jazz, LaFaro, along with his band mates in the Evans trio, helped to create a more interactive rhythm section style. Although he is most often associated with this "conversational" or "internalized beat" [45] style of playing which the Bill Evans Trio pioneered, he was well versed in several jazz bass styles, as evidenced by his work with a diverse groups of artists including Stan Kenton, Ornette Coleman, Stan Getz, Buddy Morrow, and Booker Little.[46]

Several parallels can be drawn between LaFaro's and Pettiford's lives and careers. Both came from musical families, studied several instruments before the bass, and made quick advances in the jazz world, and also died relatively young. LaFaro, however, had more formal music training. He played saxophone in the high school band, studied the first two Simandl bass method books, and briefly majored in music at Ithaca College at Ithaca, New York. [47] Although LaFaro drew inspiration directly from horn players, he was still enamored of the preceding generation of bassists, including Pettiford. [48] Eldee Young, who met and jammed with LaFaro in the late 1950's, speaks of LaFaro's admiration for Pettiford, among other bassists, when he says, "George Duvivier, Percy Heath, and Oscar Pettiford were heroes to Scotty and me."[49]

Analysis of "Hip"

"Hip," a twelve-bar blues, was recorded on Hampton Hawes' album For Real (Fantasy 1992 OJCCD-713-2). Present on this recording are Hampton Hawes, piano; Harold Land, saxophone; and Frank Butler, drums. LaFaro's solo on this tune shows many commonalities with Oscar Pettiford's style along with some key differences. This album was recorded in California in 1958, the year before LaFaro made his first studio recording with the Bill Evans Trio. LaFaro, here, displays a highly refined solo style but, as an accompanist, adheres to a more functional role.

Phrasing, and Motivic Development

As Pettiford does in the previously discussed solos, LaFaro develops his solo lines using multi-phrase statements, varying their rhythmic placement, duration and, alternating the direction of the ending interval. In mm. 1-2, LaFaro begins with a three-and-a-half beat motive beginning on the "and" of three and ending on the downbeat of two with a descending major second. This motive is suggestive of the blues sound while also accommodating the Eb7 chord in m. 2. The second phrase, mm. 3-4, is six-and-a-half beats long, beginning on the "and" of one and ending on the "and" of three with an ascending minor third. This phrase is more closely related to the blues sound, featuring both the major and minor third prominently. The third and concluding phrase, mm. 5-8, begins on the "and" of two, but lasts for twelve-and-a-half beats, ending with an ascending D min7 arpeggio, then resolving down by a half-step to the third of the G7 chord.

LaFaro, also differing from the Pettiford solos previously analyzed, develops his lines using motivic-elaboration. LaFaro develops a triplet motive using motivic-elaboration in mm. 10-16. In m. 10, LaFaro plays a fragment of the motive which features the raised fifth prominently. He follows this in mm. 11-12 with the complete motive, followed by a variation in mm. 12-13, which is phrased it across the bar-line. A second variation occurs in mm. 14-15, this time with the motive expanded in length, while a third variation occurs in mm. 15-16, diminished in length. Non-Harmonic Pitches

LaFaro, like Pettiford, also uses chromatic and altered pitches as part of his solos. LaFaro, however, employs them more frequently and in greater variety than Pettiford. This solo contains chromatic passing tones in mm.17, 25-26 and 28. He also uses chromatic lower neighbor tones in mm. 11-12, 14-15, 19-23, 25. 4.2 George Mraz

George Mraz (b.1944) is a Czechoslovakian-born bassist who has been a significant influence in American jazz since his emigration to the United States in 1968. [53] Mraz, like Pettiford, first studied other instruments, including the violin and saxophone, before switching to the bass. He received classical training on the bass in Czechoslovakia at the Prague Conservatory and later attended the Berklee College of Music in Boston on scholarship. [54] He is known for his clear tone, remarkable intonation and virtuosic technique.

Mraz, who considers Oscar Pettiford to be "one of the greatest jazz bassist (and jazz cello players) of all time," first became aware of Pettiford's reputation while working with several former associates of Pettiford, including saxophonist Stan Getz, pianist Tommy Flanagan, and guitarist Attila Zoller. [55] Although drawing inspiration from many sources, Mraz admits to being influenced by many aspects of Pettiford's playing including "his sound, time, conception, and his ability to fit into many styles of music, which at the time wasn't really everybody's strong point." [56]

Mraz's use of steel strings, low string height, and electronic amplification allow him to perform fleet solos at faster tempos than Pettiford could have achieved with the one-finger pizzicato technique and bass setup of the 1940's and 50's. The added sustain of the steel strings and his use of left-hand pizzicato and two finger pizzicato technique allow him to play legato articulations that would be difficult or impossible with the bass setup that most bassists used in the 1950's. George Mraz's solo on Charlie Parker's "Confirmation," was recorded with in 1977 with pianist Tommy Flanagan and drummer Elvin Jones on the album Eclypso (Enja 2088). Mraz's solo on this tune is an excellent example of the translation of the bebop vocabulary onto the bass.

Non-harmonic Pitches

Mraz, like Pettiford, makes use of chromatic passing tones to connect his lines. In this solo, however, he uses them much more frequently: twenty-seven times throughout his two choruses (sixty-four measures). [57] In m.4, Mraz uses a descending chromatic passing tone as part of a bebop cliché phrase. Mraz, in contrast to Pettiford, who mainly used chromatic passing tones as part of descending lines, employs chromatic passing tones in both his ascending and descending lines.

Example 38: Ascending and descending chromatic passing tones in mm. 17-18 of "Confirmation."

Mraz, like Pettiford, also makes use of chromatic neighbor tones. Mraz favors the lower which he uses nine times, in mm.2, 14, 21, 33, 40, 49 and 54-55.

Example 39: Chromatic lower neighbor tone in m. 21 of "Confirmation."

In contrast, he only uses a chromatic upper neighbor tone three times, in mm.5, 13 and 39. Mraz combines all three in mm.5-6.

Example 40: Chromatic lower neighbor and chromatic passing tones in mm. 5-6 of "Confirmation."

Altered pitches

Mraz also makes just two clear uses of altered pitches in this solo, the flatted fifth or raised fourth in m.8, and the flatted ninth as an upper chromatic neighbor tone in m. 41. Pettiford also favored using the flatted ninth in this manner.

4.3 John Patitucci

John Patitucci (b.1959) is a prominent bassist, composer and professor of music at the City College of New York. He performs on both the double bass and the six string electric bass. Classically trained, he is renowned for his virtuosity and technique on both instruments. He first came to prominence performing on electric bass with Chick Corea's Elektric Band, one of the leading fusion groups of the 1980's. He has since performed with many respected jazz musicians, including Wayne Shorter, John Scofield, and Michael Brecker. Although on his earlier recordings he possessed very bright and ringing tone, his sound has become darker in recent years, due, in part to his use of newer varieties of bass strings made to emulate the sound of gut strings. Amplification systems and pickups for the double bass have also improved greatly since the 1970's and 1980's, allowing a more naturally resonant and woody tone to be projected.

Patitucci, although aware of Pettiford, was instead drawn to bassists Ray Brown, Ron Carter, Percy Heath and Paul Chambers as major influences.[59] Thus, any initial Pettiford influence would have occurred indirectly as Brown, Chambers, and Carter all "came up" under Pettiford's influence. In the early 1990's, well after he was established as a jazz artist, Patitucci rediscovered Pettiford's music. Patitucci was "blown away by his flexibility, virtuosity and just his energy…. I was intrigued by his playing but didn't want to copy it….I did learn a few things, not formal transcriptions." [60] He was, however, inspired to learn his music. In 2001, Patitucci recorded Pettiford's "Bohemia After Dark" on his album entitled Communion (Concord Records, 2001). Patitucci cites Sonny Rollin's Freedom Suite, on which Oscar Pettiford played, as one of his motivations to recordRemembrance, his recent piano-less trio album. [61]

Summary

Each of the three bassists analyzed in this chapter possesses a style that, though keeping with the general melodic "horn-like" solo concept espoused by Pettiford, retains a distinctive and personal approach. Since Pettiford's lifetime, jazz has continues to evolve and accordingly, each bassist has incorporated new harmonic and melodic approaches into their playing.

Scott LaFaro, the earliest of the three modern bassists analyzed, is the only player who was active in the New York City jazz scene during Pettiford's lifetime. Like Pettiford, he used multi-measure phrases and flowing eighth-note lines in his solos. LaFaro, however, made use of triplets more extensively than Pettiford, and used model-sequence technique to develop his motives. LaFaro, in addition to utilizing the high register of the bass, makes a more extensive and varied use of chromaticism than Pettiford. LaFaro's expansion of the melodic "horn-like" bass solo is aided by his superior technique, lower string height and his use of two-finger pizzicato technique.

George Mraz, who has spent a great deal of time performing with Pettiford's former associates and is an admirer of Pettiford's work, adheres most closely in "Confirmation" to Pettiford's bebop oriented conception. Like Pettiford, Mraz constructs his solos from flowing eighth-note lines, employs chromatic devices common to bebop, and marks the form of the tune with lyrical ideas and recurring motives. Mraz, however, develops his motives using model sequence technique and makes much greater use of chromaticism than Pettiford. Mraz also expands Pettiford's concept into the high register of the bass and employs the legato phrasing and two-finger pizzicato technique characteristic of modern bassists.

John Patitucci, the most recent of the three bassists studied, has a style that, while still reflecting Pettiford's "horn-like" concept, applies a looser and more angular approach. Patitucci, like Pettiford uses recurring motives and lyrical ideas to create a sense of structure in his solo, however, relies less overtly on the typical elements of the bebop language. This is perhaps more reflective of Patitucci's early experience performing with fusion oriented ensembles. Patitucci, like LaFaro and Mraz, makes extensive use of the high register of the bass in his solo.

Each of these bassists have had several advantages not available to Pettiford: each received formal music training, utilized relatively low string height, and with the exception of LaFaro, [62] utilized steel strings and electronic amplification to achieve a bright and sustaining sound. These advantages allowed players develop the "horn-like" melodic solo approach to levels that technically surpassed Pettiford's work in the 1940's and 1950's.

Conclusion

Oscar Pettiford clearly stood out among his peers in the mid-1940's and 1950's as one of the first bassists to adapt the bebop vocabulary to the double bass. In doing this, Pettiford earned the admiration of numerous bassists including Ray Brown, Charles Mingus and Paul Chambers. Pettiford, after Jimmy Blanton, was one of the most important figures in establishing the bass as a capable and respected solo instrument in jazz.

Pettiford's work as a bassist, alone, would probably have been sufficient to distinguish him from others during the bebop era, but his work as a composer and bandleader was also remarkable. The bassist-bandleader was and still is a relatively unusual figure in jazz. In these capacities, Pettiford set musical precedents for such groups as those of Charles Mingus, Paul Chambers, and, more recently, John Patitucci, Dave Holland, and Avishai Cohen. As the leader of his own groups, Pettiford gave himself a platform from which to highlight and develop his talent on an instrument known primarily it's supporting role. These settings also allowed him to perform on the cello, which he used as a higher pitched extension of his bass.

The fact that the American Federation of Musicians' recording ban prohibited any recorded documentation of Pettiford's participation in the earliest bebop ensembles has caused his contributions to early bebop ensembles be unnoticed by many. His expatriation to Europe, and premature death in 1960, were factors that further vitiated his reputation among many musicians.

It is quite possible that Pettiford's greatest influence on the jazz world today has been indirect. Among many others influenced by Pettiford, some discussed in more detail above, is Ray Brown, who achieved prominence with the Oscar Peterson trio in the 1950's and 1960's, Paul Chambers and Ron Carter, who gained exposure with Miles Davis' groups. Scores of influential and accomplished bassists, including Sam Jones, Buster Williams, George Mraz, Ray Brown, Chuck Israels and Larry Grenadier have given Pettiford credit for influencing their sound and style in some way. It is significant, further, that Pettiford's music is now a required part of the repertoire for many aspiring jazz bass students.

Pettiford's extensive discography as a sideman and a leader has been reissued and is now widely available in various formats. This has allowed more recent generations of bassists to be informed by his playing. A clear and accurate discography of his recorded output, however, does not exist; and no major written work has exclusively devoted to his historical contributions to jazz.

This study of Oscar Pettiford's solo style and its relation to that of other bassists of the bebop era show that for his time, Pettiford had few peers. The analysis of the music of Scott LaFaro, George Mraz and John Patitucci give evidence that Pettiford's solo style contained many elements which would later become part of the modern bass sound. Although Pettiford's approach is not explicitlycopied by any of these bassists, each of them builds on elements of the "horn-like" solo concept that Pettiford championed in the 1940's and 1950's.
