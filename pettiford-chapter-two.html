Chapter II: Analysis of Pettiford/'s Solos


Overview

           In the history of the double bass as a solo instrument in jazz, Oscar Pettiford was a bridge linking the work of Jimmy Blanton and the generations of jazz bassists that followed him. Pettiford stood out from many of his contemporaries in the 1940's and 1950's, building on Blanton's accomplishments as a soloist and discarding the heavier root-oriented approach to soloing typical of many of his contemporaries. Pettiford was able to both translate and transfer much of the vocabulary and conventions of bebop including the use of chromaticism, "horn-like" phrasing, scales/arpeggios and altered pitches to the bass.

 

2.1: "Burt's Pad"

             This solo on Burt's Pad is included on disc two of the collection Oscar Rides Again (Proluxe 5002). It was recorded on March 13, 1954. Present with Pettiford on this recording are Kai Winding, trombone; Al Cohn, tenor sax; Henri Renaud, piano; Tal Farlow, guitar; and Max Roach, drums.

 

Phrasing

            One of the most remarkable aspects of Pettiford's solos is his phrasing. In this solo, as is characteristic of his style, Pettiford uses "horn-like" lines consisting primarily of eighth-notes, which he builds into lengthy multi-measure statements. In mm. 1-4, Pettiford plays two phrases, each two measures long. The third and concluding phrase, mm. 5-9, is five measures long and overlaps the beginning of the second "A" section.

 

Example 1: Phrasing in mm. 1-9 of "Burt's Pad."

 

Avoidance of Roots and Fifths as Prominent Pitches in Solos

            By his avoidance of the heavy use of roots and fifths, Pettiford breaks with the common tendency of bassists to outline the same pitches required of walking bass lines. This, along with the inclusion of the upper extensions of the harmony, often within arpeggios, gives his solos harmonic depth, and a sense of motion often lacked by other solo bassists during his time.

            In this solo, Pettiford avoids placing the root or fifth of the harmony on the downbeats of chord changes. In m. 1, he plays the major seventh two times, on the downbeat of beats one and three, while placing the root on the upbeat of one and the fifth on the upbeat of two. In m. 3, Pettiford plays the second (or ninth) on beat one, then outlines the C Major chord from the third to the ninth. The third phrase, although it begins in m. 4 with a root, anticipates the following measure's harmony by half of a beat causing the ninth to land on the downbeat of m. 5. Pettiford does however, use chord roots prominently in m. 4 along with a flatted ninth as a chromatic upper neighbor.

 

Example 2: Seventh and ninth as prominent pitches in m. 1-5 of "Burt's Pad."

 

Lyrical Ideas

            When the harmony of the tune shifts to a more distantly related chord, Pettiford uses lyrical ideas that clearly express that harmony using the lower chord tones. In mm. 2, 10, 26 and 34, Pettiford uses a similar lyrical idea over the Ab6 harmony that emphasizes the root and major sixth.


Example 3: Lyrical idea in mm. 2, 10, 26 and 34 of "Burt's Pad."

In mm. 16-18 Pettiford also marks the transition to the bridge with a lyrical idea that features the root, third and fifth of the new key center.

 

Example 4: Lyrical idea in mm. 16-18 of Burt's Pad.

2.2: Analysis of "Perdido"

Overview

            "Perdido" from Oscar Rides Again (Proluxe 5002), was recorded in New York City on June 12, 1956, by Pettiford's big band the Oscar Pettiford Orchestra. In this solo, as is characteristic of his general approach, Pettiford employs long eighth-note lines, arpeggios, chromaticism and motivic development.

 

Phrasing

            In this solo, as in "Burt's Pad, Pettiford achieves a sense of fluidity by using long, flowing phrasing. In mm. 1-7 of this solo, Pettiford employs a three-phrase statement, clearly punctuating the end of each phrase with a dotted quarter note in mm. 3, 5, 7. The first two phrases of this statement occur in mm. 1-3 and mm. 3-5, and are each eight beats long, beginning on the �and� of two and ending on the downbeat of beat one. The third and concluding phrase, in mm. 5-7, while also beginning on the "and" of two, is two beats longer and ends on an upbeat. Pettiford also varies his phrase endings by changing the direction and interval in each case. The first phrase ends with a descending minor second interval, the second an ascending major second and the third with an ascending minor third.

 

 

 

 

 

Example 10:  Phrase development in mm. 1-7 of  "Perdido.

 

Recurring Motives

            Pettiford also creates cohesion in this solo by using recurring motives. In mm. 1-2, 5-6, and 13 he plays an arpeggio figure over the C min7 that includes the third, fifth, seventh and ninth. In mm. 2 and 6, he appears to extend the Cmin7 harmony until beat three of the measure when he sounds the pitch A, the third of the F7 chord.

 

Example 11: Recurring motive over Cmin7 in mm. 1-2, 5-6 and 13 of "Perdido.""

 

            Pettiford uses a similar descending motive three times in this solo, over G7 in m. 8, F7 in m. 10, and Cmin7 in m. 29. Consistent with the phrase development just discussed, Pettiford augments the phrase length and changes the ending interval in each use. He also moves the motive closer to the beginning of the measure each time he uses it. In the first occurrence of this motive, mm. 8-9, the phrase is six beats long and ends with a descending minor second. The second, in mm. 10-11, is six and a half beats long and ends with an ascending major second. The third and concluding iteration of this motive, mm. 29-32, occurs near the end of the form. It is part of a four-measure phrase that ends with a descending minor second.

 

Example 12: First instance of descending motive in mm. 8-9 of "Perdido."

 

Example 13: Second instance of recurring descending motive in mm. 10-11 of "Perdido."

 

Example 14: Third and concluding instance of recurring descending motive in mm. 29-32 of "Perdido."

 

Arpeggios as a Recurring Motive

            Pettiford plays a similar motive over both the Dmin7 and G7 chords in mm. 4, 12, and 32. In m. 4, he arpeggiates the lower three tones of the Dmin7 chord then plays the flatted ninth (as an upper chromatic neighbor), fifth, root and seventh of G7. In m. 12 Pettiford plays the complete descending Dmin7 arpeggio followed the same G7 pattern as m. 4. In m. 32, he appears to anticipate the G7 by two beats with third, fifth, seventh and flatted ninth used again as an upper chromatic neighbor tone.

 

Example 15: Recurring motive in mm. 4, 12, 32 in "Perdido."

 

Lyrical Idea Marking Form of Tune

            Although Pettiford overlaps a phrase across the A and B sections of the tune, he marks the arrival of the bridge in m. 17 by playing a lyrical idea containing the root, third, and fifth of the D7 chord. He also does something similar to this in his solo on "Burt's Pad"

 

Example 16: Lyrical phrase outlining the bridge in mm. 14-17 of  "Perdido."

 

Non-Harmonic Tones

            As discussed previously in the analysis of "Burt's Pad," Pettiford's solos stand out because of his use of chromatic passing tones to create smooth, flowing solo lines. In this solo, Pettiford employs nine chromatic passing tones, each of which occurs only as part of descending lines. These passing tones occur in mm. 2, 5, 8, 9, 10, 11, 21, 29 and 30.

 

Example 17: Chromatic passing tones in mm. 29-30 of "Perdido."

 

Blues language

            Pettiford implies the blues sound with a Db, the flatted third of the key, in m. 14. Although this pitch is also the raised fifth of the prevailing F7 harmony, Pettiford slides into it from below implying the Bb blues sound. He returns to the Bb blues sound again in m. 18, playing both the major and minor (flatted) third of Bb over the D7 harmony. In example 19, Pettiford plays an Eb7 arpeggio over the F7 harmony in m. 24. The resulting pitches, G, Bb, and Db, clearly introduce the blues language motive that Pettiford then develops through repetition in mm. 25-28. The use of blues language on non-blues tunes is a practice also associated with bassist Ray Brown, who listed Pettiford as one of the two greatest influences on his music, the other being Jimmy Blanton.[39]

 

Example 18: Blues language in mm. 18-19 of "Perdido.""

Summary

            Pettiford, as evidenced in these solos, possessed a highly developed solo style. In his solos he broke free from many of the melodic patterns associated with bass lines, incorporating many of the devices associated with the bebop horn players of his day: multi-measure eighth-note phrases, arpeggios, chromatic passing tones, chromatic upper and lower neighbor tones, and altered tones. The fact that he performed on other instruments before the bass may have been a factor leading to his adoption of the "horn-like" approach to soloing on the bass. Pettiford's ability to translate the vocabulary of bebop to the bass is even more remarkable considering his lack of formal training on the instrument. In Pettiford's time, relatively few jazz bassists had access to formal instruction; this lack of technical training undoubtedly hindered many from developing sophisticated solo styles. Furthermore, the instrument's setup prior to the development of electronic amplification and steel strings created enormous difficulties in projecting the sound of the bass through an ensemble. Much of bass technique at this time focused on producing adequate volume at the expense of other qualities.

            Pettiford's ability to achieve a relatively high level of musical fluency on the instrument, despite his lack of training and the inherent physical difficulties presented by the bass, is a testament to his innate musical abilities. His solos, which are remarkable on their own merits, stand out even further when compared to the solos of many of his contemporaries in the bebop scene of the late 1940's and early 1950's.
