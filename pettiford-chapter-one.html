Chapter 1: Background

 

Early Life (1922-1943)

            Oscar Pettiford (1922-1960) was born on an Indian reservation in Okmulgee, Oklahoma, into a large musical family. [1] Pettiford received his early musical training and musical experience in the family band, Doc Pettiford and His Family Orchestra. The band featured his father, "Doc" Pettiford, and included his mother and his ten siblings. [2] Pettiford began dancing with the band at age six, later playing the piano, trombone and trumpet before taking up the bass. [3] Pettiford was educated in the basics of music theory by his older sister, Leontine, who played piano and reeds and who arranged most of the band's music. [4] The family band toured the southern and midwestern United States before eventually basing itself in Minneapolis, Minnesota, where Pettiford was raised.

            Pettiford encountered many influential musicians at after-hours jam sessions in Minneapolis. It was in one of these sessions where he met Milt Hinton, bassist with Cab Calloway,[5] jammed with the electric guitar pioneer Charlie Christian, and had a "cutting contest" with Jimmy Blanton, bassist with the Duke Ellington Orchestra and the progenitor of the modern jazz bass solo. [6]

             In the early 1940's, musical work was scarce in Minneapolis. Pettiford briefly gave up professional music and went to work in a war plant while studying tailoring. He returned to professional music at the urging of bassist Milt Hinton. [7] Pettiford's first major opportunity in jazz came in 1943 when saxophonist and bandleader Charlie Barnet hired him to be part of a two-bass team with Chubby Jackson. Pettiford only stayed with Barnet for five months, however, and this role was undocumented because of an American Federation of Musicians' recording ban in effect at the time. [8] He was dismissed when his competitive nature and abrasive personality combined to cause friction between himself and bassist Chubby Jackson. [9] After his departure from Barnet's band, Pettiford relocated  and settled in New York City. [10] It was here that he immediately became part of the nascent bebop scene, which first developed in Harlem and then later took hold at several nightclubs located on 52nd  Street, between 5th and 6th avenue.

 

Early Career in New York City (1943-1945)

            In New York, Pettiford's first gig was with pianist and bebop pioneer Thelonious Monk at Minton's Playhouse in Harlem. Minton's Playhouse was the setting for some of the earliest bebop jam sessions attended by musicians such as Dizzy Gillespie, Charlie Parker, Jimmy Blanton and Charlie Christian. In June of 1943, Pettiford worked for a short time at the Onyx Club on 52nd Street with famed trumpeter Roy Eldridge, who ultimately fired Pettiford because of[11] his constant tardiness and his drinking. [12]

            Notwithstanding these setbacks, Pettiford's reputation as a bassist grew quickly, and by the end of 1943 he had won first place in the bass category of Esquire magazine's All-American Jazz Band Poll. [13] The winners of this poll, which included Louis Armstrong, Jack Teagarden, Sid Catlett, and Coleman Hawkins, performed a concert that was recorded and released as The First Esquire Concert (Laser Light 15 723).

            Due largely to the success of the Esquire award, Pettiford was able to book a gig on 52nd Street at the Onyx Club as co-leader with trumpeter Dizzy Gillespie, pianist George Wallington, tenor saxophonist Don Byas and drummer Max Roach. [14] This group, which was also unrecorded, is now recognized as the first bebop ensemble on 52nd Street, a strip of nightclubs that would become, for a short time, a bastion for bebop musicians. Pettiford took a direct role in the musical direction of this band, and according to Budd Johnson, a saxophonist who performed with the group, half of this group's repertoire was written by Pettiford. "One Bass Hit, which was then known as "For Bass Faces Only," was included among these works. [15]

In 1944, after just a few months of work in New York City, Pettiford"s heavy drinking and confrontational personality caused a falling-out between him and Gillespie, which, in turn, caused the breakup of the ensemble. Following that band's dissolution, Pettiford led various bands on 52nd Street, eventually playing and recording with The Boyd Raeburn Band (1944-45) [16] [17] before traveling to California to perform with legendary saxophonist Coleman Hawkins. Pettiford appeared with Hawkins in the 1945 film, The Crimson Canary, although another bassist overdubbed the bass part heard in the movie. [18] Pettiford also recorded as a leader of an eighteen-piece group in 1945 for the Manor record label under the name Oscar Pettiford and His 18 All-Stars. [19] 

 

Time with Duke Ellington (1945-1948)

In November of 1945, Pettiford was hired to fill the bass chair in the Duke Ellington Orchestra, succeeding bassist Jimmy Blanton, who died in 1942. [20] Pettiford performed and recorded with Ellington from 1945 through March of 1948, returning to the ensemble after that for occasional performances. [21] Near the end of his time with the Ellington band, however, Pettiford grew restless with them, only enjoying the concerts "because then we'd get to play some new things. At dances and theaters we'd play the same things over and over again. There was nothing to inspire you."" [22]

 

1948-1950

            After leaving Ellington, Pettiford worked at The Three Deuces on 52nd Street first with pianist Erroll Garner and later with pianist George Shearing, trombonist Kai Winding, and trumpeter Miles Davis (1948). [23] Pettiford also led a short-lived band at The Clique nightclub that included Miles Davis, Fats Navarro, Lucky Thompson, Dexter Gordon, Milt Jackson, Bud Powell and Kenny Clarke. [24] This ensemble also disbanded as a result of conflict between Pettiford and the other musicians.[25]

             In 1949, Pettiford joined Woody Herman's "Second Herd" band. During his time with the Herman band, Pettiford broke his arm playing baseball. Before he broke his arm but during his time with Herman, Pettiford experimented with the cello as part of a prank on the bandleader. After his arm was broken, he was unable to play bass due to the cast. During this time, Pettiford instead devoted his energy to practicing the cello, which he tuned in fourths and played pizzicato, essentially as a smaller bass sounding an octave higher than the conventional jazz bass.

 

Bandleader and Sideman (1950-1958)      

            During the early 1950's Pettiford continued working as a bandleader, engaging in an ill-fated State Department tour and making recordings on both bass and cello for Charles Mingus's Debut record label, the Roost record label, and others. [26] During this period, Pettiford also worked with Charlie Shavers and Louis Bellson (1953) and in Bud Powell's trio (1953). [27]

            In 1955, Pettiford was hired to lead a band at the Cafe Bohemia. Pettiford's group was a last minute replacement for one led by Charlie Parker, who died before his scheduled opening. [28] Pettiford led a band that included trombonist Jimmy Cleveland, saxophonist Jerome Richardson, pianist Horace Silver, drummer Kenny Clarke and alto saxophonist Julian "Cannonball" Adderley, who was discovered after Pettiford was tricked into letting him onto the bandstand to sit in. [29]

             Pettiford recorded with Miles Davis on his 1955 album, The Musings of Miles (Original Jazz Classics OJC-004). The band featured on this recording, minus Pettiford, would eventually form the nucleus of Davis� classic mid-fifties quintet. According to Red Garland, Davis originally sought to have Garland, Oscar Pettiford, Sonny Rollins and Max Roach make up his working quintet.[30] When Davis was ready to put his own band together, however, Pettiford, Rollins and Roach were already working with their own groups and were, therefore, unavailable.[31] Paul Chambers, a gifted young bassist inspired by both Blanton and Pettiford was instead hired. Chambers would remain with Davis's groups for eight years and in this position influence countless other bassists.

            From the mid 1950's until leaving for Europe in 1958, Pettiford recorded prolifically both as a leader and as a sideman with both large ensembles and numerous small combos.  In 1956, Pettiford assembled a thirteen-piece big band, which debuted at New York's Town Hall. This band, the Oscar Pettiford Orchestra, played together for eighteen months and featured a group of seasoned musicians that included Ernie Royal, Art Farmer, Jimmy Cleveland, Gig Gryce, Lucky Thompson, Benny Golson, Art Farmer, Tommy Flanagan, David Amram, and Osie Johnson, among others. [32] This group featured Pettiford on both the cello (which in some instances was amplified with an added electronic tremolo effect) and bass, at times simultaneously via overdubbing. This group also expanded the traditional instrumentation of the jazz big band by employing two French horns and a harpist as regular members.

            As a sideman, Pettiford contributed to several notable recordings during this time, including Thelonious Monk's Monk Plays Ellington (1955), Brilliant Corners (1956) and Sonny Rollins' Freedom Suite, (1958) which featured Rollins, Pettiford and Max Roach as a piano-less trio.

 

 

Final Years in Europe (1958-1960)

            Pettiford left New York City for Europe in 1958 as part of a Jazz from Carnegie Hall package tour.[33] He toured France and Germany and eventually settled in Copenhagen, Denmark. [34] In Copenhagen Pettiford worked with such other American expatriate jazz musicians as Stan Getz, Kenny Clarke, and Bud Powell as well as with various European musicians, including guitarist Attilla Zoller, trumpeter Alan Botschinky, pianist Jan Johansson, and drummer Hans Koller. [35] [36] According to bassist Dave Friesen, the Danish jazz bass virtuoso Niels-Henning Orsted Peterson, who lived in Copenhagen, mentioned studying with Pettiford. [37] It was in Copenhagen during 1959 and 1960 that Pettiford made some of his final recordings, issued as Montmartre Blues, using a band comprised of European musicians. Oscar Pettiford died on September 8, 1960, in Copenhagen, Denmark, from a  "flu-like virus" following a concert at an art gallery. [38]
